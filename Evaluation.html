Evaluation Tests

Certain Evaluation tests we could possibly conduct are -

  • Checking for uneven acceleration on the three different motors - An uneven acceleration in the three motors will cause the robot to tilt which would mean that the robot won't be able to climb the pole and could possibly also result in disintegration. A possible way to test for unequal acceleration is to use an accelerometer for the motors and check to ensure they have equal accelerations.
  • We need to regulate the speed of the motors for ideal exposure of the pole to the UVC. According to a research conducted by our team member Yasmin Yang for ideal exposure of the pole to the UVC LED we would require a dosage of 10.6 mJ/cm2 according to the source UV-study in addition to which we would have an irradiance of 1.17 mW/cm2, therefore, we calculated exposure time as Exposure = Dosage / Irradiance which on calculation is equivalent to 9 seconds. The ideal speed of the motor was then calculated to be 0.22 rad/s. Therefore, in order to achieve ideal exposure we will have to ensure that each motor moves at a constant speed of 0.22 rad/s. We would probably do so using a speedometer.
  • Additionally, we can try a variety of heights of the pole to test on, just to make sure that we achieve a successful implementation for a wide range of heights and the robot does not experience any issues climbing large distances.
  • A useful evaluation test would be to add noise to the sensors in a webots simulation.
  • It would also be a good idea to test the webots simulation with different values of friction between the wheels and the pole that the robot will climb.
  • As far as webots simulations are concerned, it would also be a good idea to test our robot for different speeds of our motor apart from 0.22 rad/s.
  • Another evaluation test that could possibly be useful is observing and ensuring that there is minimal wear and tear of the robot wheels and motors after repeated use so that we don't end up changing the friction between the pole and the wheels.

Main Areas of Improvement

The main problem we are experiencing right now is due to unequal acceleration between the motors due to which PAUL is not able to climb the pole ideally. Once the hardware team is able to access the robot, they should be able to work out the cause for this by measuring the accelerations of all the three motors and working out the reason for unequal accelerations. Therefore, the main area of improvement right now would be to simply correct the unequal accelerations.
